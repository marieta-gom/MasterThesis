Human embryonic kidney (HEK293) cells are widely used for producing complex biotherapeutics requiring human-like post-translational modifications. In intensified perfusion bioprocesses, maintaining high viable cell densities over extended cultivation times requires robust real-time monitoring strategies aligned with Process Analytical Technology (PAT) principles promoted by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration. However, key metabolic variables such as oxygen uptake rate (OUR) are difficult to measure directly in continuous mammalian systems, motivating model-based soft sensors using non-invasive off-gas analysis.

This thesis presents the development and evaluation of an off-gas-based soft sensor for metabolic monitoring of HEK293 perfusion cultures at 100 L scale. OUR was determined through a global gas-phase oxygen mass balance using differential O$_2$ concentrations between bioreactor inlet and exhaust streams under constant dissolved oxygen (DO) control. Because mammalian cultures exhibit small oxygen concentration differences, the resulting OUR signal showed a low signal-to-noise ratio and oscillatory behavior driven by the DO proportional–integral–derivative (PID) controller.

Initial smoothing with a Savitzky–Golay filter reduced variance but insufficiently separated controller dynamics from biological behavior. To address nonlinearities and uncertainty, three Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF) formulations of increasing complexity were developed: (v1) a data-driven smoothing estimator, (v2) a physically constrained gas-balance observer, and (v3) a control-aware grey-box observer integrating process dynamics and stochastic flexibility. The final formulation provided the best balance between noise attenuation, physical consistency, and uncertainty representation.

The v3 UKF was formulated as a control-aware state observer applied to raw gas-phase measurements, incorporating manipulated oxygen input, inlet–outlet signal coupling, gas-phase delay effects, and PID actuator and analyzer dynamics. OUR was modeled as a soft-constrained state reconstructed through gas-balance-driven dynamics from filtered oxygen fractions rather than as an independent variable, while controller-driven oscillations were treated as structured disturbances assuming oxygen-saturated mitochondrial respiration at the applied 50\% DO setpoint.

The developed soft sensor enabled stable estimation of OUR and cell-specific oxygen uptake rates ($qO_2$) under high-cell-density perfusion conditions. Sensitivity limitations in historical low-biomass datasets highlighted the dependence of gas-phase mass balance methods on sufficient respiratory signal strength. In parallel, $qO_2$ was analyzed with glucose uptake ($q_{Gluc}$), lactate production ($q_{Lac}$), and lactate-to-glucose yield ($Y_{Lac/Gluc}$) to investigate metabolic dynamics. During ramp-up, oxygen consumption showed nonlinear relationships with substrate uptake and byproduct formation, whereas during production, $q_{Gluc}$ and $q_{Lac}$ remained relatively stable despite increasing oxygen consumption, suggesting metabolic decoupling and a shift toward a more respiratory phenotype.

Overall, this work establishes a methodological foundation for non-invasive OUR estimation in intensified mammalian perfusion bioprocesses and a framework for evaluating signal decomposition, controller dynamics, and uncertainty propagation in soft-sensor development for continuous biomanufacturing applications.
